i-stems

Certain third-declension nouns and all third-declension adjectives are i-stems, which stick a couple of random i's near the end of inflected forms. i-stems are extremely inconsistent‚Äînot every rule listed is always followed.


Typically, genitive plurals ends in -ium instead of um, neuter plurals (nominative and accusative) end in -ia instead of -a, neuter ablative singulars end in -i, and sometimes there are other changes. For an in-depth explanation and a discussion of pure and mixed i-stems, Allen and Greenough from Dickinson College Commentaries is a good source (keep clicking the next arrow).

Pluralia Tantum

Certain words only appear in the plural even though they may refer to singular entities. For instance, we do not say a single pant, only pants. In Latin, such a word is termed plurale tantum and include Athenae (Athens), gratiae (thanks), and liberi (children).

Singularia Tantum

Similar to plurale tantum, a word is termed singularia tantum if it exists solely in the singular. It is pretty hard to have waters (so, Latin only has aqua), milks (hence, lac), and hungers (so, fames).

Greek Loanwords

Since Latin was heavily influenced by Greek, there are some weird words that were loaned from Greek but were not fully assimilated into Latin. For instance, there are the first declension masculine occupations auriga, poeta, pirata, incola, agricola, and nauta (APPIAN). Many names also have strange forms (e.g., the accusative of Aeneas is Aenean).

Heteroclites

Certain words have some forms from one declension and other forms from a different declension, switching back and forth. For instance, domus (house) is very weird because you can choose which declension to use. Meanwhile, vas (vessil) and virus (virus) are third-declension in the singular but second-declension in the plural.

Heterogeneous Nouns

These are nouns that change their gender when changing from singular to plural. For instance, epulum, n. (feast, banquet) changes to the feminine epulae in the plural. There are other words, like balneum occur in the plural as either feminine or neuter. Still other words occur of the second declension occur in either the neuter or the masculine.

Nouns Different in the Plural

Certain nouns have different meanings in the singular and plural. Wikipedia maintains a nice list.

Defective Nouns

These nouns lack forms in certain cases and numbers, making them very easy to decline (you don't), but pretty difficult to work with. For instance, vesper (evening) doesn't really have a genitive. See Allen and Greenough from Dickinson College Commentaries for more information.

Other Irregular Nouns

There are still more nouns that look pretty scary, like bos, bovis, m./f. ‚Äî cow and sus, suis, m./f. ‚Äî pig, which both occasionally appear with some interesting forms. We also sometimes see filiabus and deabus for the ablative/dative plurals of filia and dea, for their normal ablative/dative plural would be filiis or deis, which would be indistinguishable from the masculine form. But, deus also has its own irregularities. Then, vis, f. ‚Äî force has no real genitive or dative, while vicis, f. is missing the nominative and some other forms. Finally, some Greek words are also weird like lexis, lexeos, f. ‚Äî word.

Indeclinable Nouns

Indeclinable nouns have only one pattern that remains the same regardless of the case. See Allen and Greenough from Dickinson College Commentaries for more details.

Gerundives

The gerundive is essentially the future passive participle (which is why clicking on the gerundive checkbox is the same as clicking on the future passive participle checkbox). The gerundive denotes necessity, obligation, or propriety and is often used in constructions such as Carthago delenda est. See Allen and Greenough from Dickinson College Commentaries for more details.

Gerunds

The gerund expresses an action of the verb in the form of a verbal noun. As a noun the gerund is itself governed by other words; as a verb it may take an object in the proper case. Gerunds act as genitives, datives, accusatives, or ablative (always neuter) nouns, while the nominative noun form of a verb is supplied by the infinitive. See Allen and Greenough from Dickinson College Commentaries for more details.

Supines

The supine is a verbal abstract of the 4th declension, having no distinction of tense or person, and limited to two uses. First, the form in -um is the accusative of the end of motion (to express purpose). Second, the form in -u is usually the dative of purpose, but the ablative was early confused with it (so we call it the ablative supine). The ablative supine denotes an action in reference to which the quality is asserted. See Allen and Greenough from Dickinson College Commentaries for more details.

Adjective Degree

In English, happy, happier, and happiest all express the same concept, but to different degrees. If you are happy, the happiness exists but you cannot say much about it, so we call this form positive (it's there). If you are happier, you need to be happier than something else, so we call this comparative. But, the adjective happiest is superlative. Latin does the same. See Allen and Greenough from Dickinson College Commentaries for more details.

Adjective Degree

Adjectives fall into two major declensions, 1st + 2nd and 3rd. The first category has 1st declension endings for feminine nouns, but 2nd declension endings for masculine and neuter nouns. The second category uses 3rd declension endings, but is split into categories based on termination. See Allen and Greenough from Dickinson College Commentaries for more details.

Pronominal Adjectives

Certain adjectives are pronominal not only because they have pronoun-like meanings (e.g., alius, alia, aliud ‚Äî the other is sort of like a pronoun) but that they decline as pronouns do. Specifically, these nine adjectives end with -ius in the genitive singular and -i in the dative singular. LatinTutorial has a great video.
