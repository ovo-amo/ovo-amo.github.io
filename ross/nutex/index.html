A note on the name. \(\TeX\) was created by Donald Knuth and takes its name from the Greek τεχνη (technē), "art" or "craft" — the same root as technical. The final letter is a Greek chi (χ), not an "x", so \(\TeX\) is pronounced "tech" (as in technology), never "teks". \(\LaTeX\) stands for Lamport TeX, after Leslie Lamport, who built it as a friendlier layer on top of \(\TeX\); say it "LAH-tek" or "LAY-tek". Writing the logos with their characteristic raised and lowered letters requires the macros \TeX and \LaTeX — you only ever need these in running text, never inside an equation.

Inline and Display Math

\(\LaTeX\) works much like any other text editor, except that you constantly switch between text mode (ordinary prose) and math mode (where letters become italic variables and spacing is handled for you).

There are two flavours of math mode. Inline math uses \(...\) and sits inside a line of text; display math uses \[...\] and is centred on its own line. Inline math is the default for almost everything, like the set of integers is denoted \(\mathbf{Z}\). Reserve display math for expressions you want to emphasise, or that are simply too tall or wide to sit comfortably in a line. For instance, the \(3\times 3\) identity matrix is best displayed: \[\begin{bmatrix}1&0&0\\0&1&0\\0&0&1\end{bmatrix}.\] Notice that the matrix above renders centred and at full size, whereas \(\mathbf{Z}\) stayed in the line — that is the difference between the two modes.

Always wrap any mathematics, even a lone number, in these delimiters (calendar dates are the usual exception, since they are not really mathematical quantities). Punctuation belongs to the sentence, not the equation, but it still must appear: a sentence that ends on display math still ends with a period, placed inside the \[...\] right after the expression. Finally, never begin a sentence with mathematics — at least one word must precede any notation, both because a capital letter cannot start an italic variable cleanly and because it simply reads better.

This platform shows you which wrapper an exercise expects on the grey lines above and below the editor, and supplies it automatically — but keep mental track of whether you are in \(...\) or \[...\], because it changes how some symbols are sized (see 2.3 and 3.1).

Symbols

Numbers and the basic operators +, -, = are typed literally. Everything else — a relation, a Greek letter, a special sign — is produced by a command (also called a macro): a backslash \ followed by a name. For example, never multiply with * (which renders as the raised asterisk \(*\)); use \cdot for a centred dot \(a\cdot b\) or \times for a cross \(a\times b\). A command name is made of letters only, so \(\LaTeX\) needs to know where it ends: \times2 would be read as a command called times2. Either add a space (\times 2) or a brace; the space is swallowed in the output, so it never appears on the page.

Fonts and Alphabets

The same letter can be set in several mathematical alphabets, each carrying its own meaning. You saw one already: \(\mathbf{Z}\) is written \mathbf{Z}, where \mathbf stands for math bold face. The Z in braces is the command's argument — the thing it acts on. Many commands take no argument, but a font command always takes exactly one (everything between its braces is restyled).

The most important alphabet for you is blackboard bold, \mathbb, which gives \(\mathbb{R}\), \(\mathbb{N}\), \(\mathbb{Z}\), \(\mathbb{Q}\), \(\mathbb{C}\). Why "bb"? On a blackboard a lecturer cannot easily write a thick bold letter, so the convention grew of doubling one stroke of the letter instead — a "double-struck" R for the bold \(\mathbf R\) of the reals. \(\LaTeX\) named the command after that classroom habit: \math-bb = "math blackboard-bold". Remember the picture of a chalk-doubled letter and you will never forget the name. (Many number theorists at Ross will happily debate \(\mathbb{Z}\) versus \(\mathbf{Z}\); both are correct, but pick one and stay consistent within a document.)

The other alphabets are named just as literally once you know the typographic word behind each.

Superscripts, Subscripts, and Operators

Almost every formula carries an exponent, an index, or a big operator like a sum. These three subsections give you the grammar for all of them.

Superscripts and Subscripts

A superscript is produced with ^ and a subscript with _ (underscore). Each attaches to the single token before it: x^2 gives \(x^2\) and a_1 gives \(a_1\). "Single token" is the crucial idea — if the script is more than one character, you must wrap it in braces. x^10 renders as \(x^10\) (a one raised, then a stray zero), whereas x^{10} gives the intended \(x^{10}\). The same holds for subscripts: a_{ij}, not a_ij.

You can combine both on one base, and the order does not matter: x_i^2 and x^2_i both render as \(x_i^2\). What you may not do is stack two of the same kind directly: x^2^3 is the classic double superscript error. Decide what you mean and brace it — x^{2^3} for a tower \(x^{2^3}\), or {x^2}^3 for \({x^2}^3\). Nesting is fine as deep as you like, as long as every level is braced.

Two shortcuts. A prime is just an apostrophe: f' and f'' give \(f'\) and \(f''\) (it is shorthand for a superscript, so do not write f^'). And a leading subscript or superscript with an empty base, like the left-index in \({}^{n}C_k\), is written {}^{n}C_k — the empty {} gives the script something to attach to.

x^2 + y^2 Single-character exponents need no braces: x^2. a_1 + a_2 + a_n Subscripts use _: a_1, a_2, a_n. x^{10} - 1 A two-digit exponent must be braced: x^{10}, not x^10. a_{ij} = x_i^2 + y_j^2 Two-letter subscript braced: a_{ij}. A base may carry both scripts in either order: x_i^2. 2^{2^k} To stack exponents you must brace the inner one: 2^{2^k}. Writing 2^2^k is a double-superscript error. f'(x_0) A prime is a plain apostrophe right after the symbol: f'.

Large Operators

Sums, products, and integrals are large operators. Each takes its bounds as a subscript (lower) and superscript (upper), exactly like the operators-with-limits above:

\sum_{i=1}^{n} i = \frac{n(n+1)}{2}
\prod_{k=1}^{n} k = n!
\int_0^1 x^2 \, \mathrm{d}x = \frac{1}{3}

Two things to carry away. First, the integral's "d" should be upright and preceded by a thin space: write x^2 \, \mathrm{d}x, never x^2 dx. Second, all the large operators share one pattern, so once you can write a sum you can write any of them:

Operator Sizing: Display vs Inline

A large operator changes shape depending on the surrounding mode, and this is the single most common source of "why does it look squished?" confusion. The switch is governed by \(\LaTeX\)'s notion of style: display math uses \displaystyle (bounds stacked above and below, large symbol), inline math uses \textstyle (bounds tucked to the side, compact). The same code gives both:

\textstyle\sum_{i=1}^{n} i
\displaystyle\sum_{i=1}^{n} i

You can override the style per operator. Prefix with \displaystyle to force the big, stacked look inside a line — though that stretches the line and usually means the expression wants its own display. The mirror \textstyle forces the compact form even in display. For only the bound placement (without resizing) there are \limits and \nolimits after the operator, but \displaystyle is the lever you will actually reach for.

\displaystyle\sum_{k=0}^{n} \binom{n}{k} = 2^n To keep the stacked-bounds look inside a line, prefix with \displaystyle. Fractions, Roots, and Binomials

Stacked notation — fractions, radicals, binomial coefficients, and the congruences of modular arithmetic.

Fractions

The workhorse is \frac{numerator}{denominator}: \frac{a+b}{c} gives \(\frac{a+b}{c}\). Like the large operators, a fraction is sized by the current style. In display math it is full size; inline, it shrinks so the line is not pushed apart, which is why \frac{1}{2} looks small in a sentence: \(\frac{1}{2}\).

You can override the style per fraction. \dfrac{a}{b} is a display fraction, forced full-size even inline: \(\dfrac{a}{b}\). \tfrac{a}{b} is a text fraction, forced small even in display: \(\tfrac{a}{b}\). Reach for \tfrac when a small fraction appears in running prose like a coefficient, and for \dfrac when an inline fraction is genuinely unreadable at the shrunken size. For a continued fraction, \cfrac keeps every level full-size and aligned; it is occasionally handy but rarely needed in a basic document. As a rule, if you find yourself fighting the sizing inline, that is \(\LaTeX\) telling you the expression wants its own displayed line.

\frac{a + b}{c} Fraction: \frac{numerator}{denominator}. \frac{1}{2} + \frac{1}{3} = \frac{5}{6} Three fractions joined by + and =; each is a \frac{...}{...}. \frac{\mathrm{d}}{\mathrm{d}x} \left( x^n \right) = n x^{n-1} A derivative as a fraction of upright differentials: \frac{\mathrm{d}}{\mathrm{d}x}. Wrap \(x^n\) in auto-sized parentheses \left( ... \right) (see 4.2); the exponent x^{n-1} is braced. \tfrac{1}{2} Force the small inline form with \tfrac.

Square Roots and Radicals

A square root is \sqrt{...}: \sqrt{x + 1} gives \(\sqrt{x+1}\). The radical sign automatically stretches its vinculum (the bar on top) over the whole argument, so always brace the full contents — \sqrt{x+1}, not \sqrt x+1, which only covers the \(x\): \(\sqrt x+1\).

For higher roots, an optional argument in square brackets sets the index: \sqrt[3]{x} is the cube root \(\sqrt[3]{x}\), and \sqrt[n]{x} gives the \(n\)th root \(\sqrt[n]{x}\). This is your first encounter with the square-bracket optional argument, a pattern shared by several commands. Roots nest cleanly: \sqrt{1 + \sqrt{x}} gives \(\sqrt{1 + \sqrt{x}}\).

\sqrt{x + 1} Brace the entire radicand so the bar covers it all: \sqrt{x + 1}. \sqrt[3]{x} The index goes in an optional square-bracket argument: \sqrt[3]{x}. x = \frac{-b \pm \sqrt{b^2 - 4ac}}{2a} The quadratic formula: plus-or-minus \pm, a square root \sqrt{b^2 - 4ac} with braced exponent, all over 2a as one fraction. \sqrt{1 + \sqrt{x}} Roots nest: put one \sqrt{...} inside another.

Binomial Coefficients

A binomial coefficient is \binom{n}{k}, giving \(\binom{n}{k}\) — "n choose k". It is sized by style just like a fraction, with display and text variants \dbinom and \tbinom. Do not build it by hand out of \frac and parentheses; \binom spaces and sizes the parentheses for you.

\binom{n}{k} = \frac{n!}{k!\,(n-k)!} Binomial on the left: \binom{n}{k}. On the right, factorials with a thin space: \frac{n!}{k!\,(n-k)!}. (x + y)^n = \sum_{k=0}^{n} \binom{n}{k} x^{k} y^{n-k} The binomial theorem: a sum \sum_{k=0}^{n} of \binom{n}{k} times braced powers x^{k} y^{n-k}.

Modular Arithmetic

Number theory is the heart of Ross, so congruences deserve their own corner. The congruence relation is \equiv, and the parenthesised modulus is \pmod{...}, which supplies the parentheses and the word "mod" and the spacing all at once: a \equiv b \pmod{n} renders as \(a \equiv b \pmod{n}\). Contrast this with the binary \bmod from 2.2, used when "mod" is an operator inside an expression: \(a \bmod n\).

For divisibility, prefer the relation \mid over a bare |: a \mid b reads "a divides b" with proper relational spacing, \(a \mid b\), and its negation is \nmid, \(a \nmid b\). As in set-builder notation, the reason to avoid | here is spacing: \mid is a relation, a lone bar is not.

Environments

So far every expression has been a single line. An environment is a block opened by \begin{name} and closed by \end{name}, and it is how \(\LaTeX\) builds anything with rows and columns — aligned equations, matrices, and case splits. Two characters do all the structural work inside every environment in this section: & separates columns, and \\ ends a row. Master those two and the rest is just choosing a name.

Aligning Equations

A single displayed equation can simply use \[...\], but a multi-line derivation should have its equals signs lined up. That is what the align* environment does. Put one & immediately before the symbol you want every line to align on (usually =), and end each line with \\:

\begin{align*}
(a+b)^2 &= a^2 + 2ab + b^2 \\
        &= a^2 + b^2 + 2ab
\end{align*}

Read the & as an invisible vertical line the equals signs snap to. A few rules to keep in mind:

RuleWhy
One & per line, before the alignment symbolEverything left of & is right-aligned, everything right is left-aligned
End every line but the last with \\A stray \\ after the final line causes a "misplaced alignment" error
Star the name: align*, not alignThe star suppresses equation numbers (a convention used throughout \(\LaTeX\)); align numbers every line
Type only the body hereThe editor supplies the \begin{align*} / \end{align*} wrapper for you, just as it supplies \(...\)
x + y &= 5 \\ x - y &= 1 Two equations aligned on =: put & right before each equals sign and end the first line with \\. f(x) &= (x+1)^2 \\ &= x^2 + 2x + 1 Align on = with &=. The second line has nothing left of the equals sign, so it begins with &. Brace the exponent: (x+1)^2. a &= b \\ b &= c \\ a &= c Three aligned lines; each ends with \\ except the last, and each has & before its =. \binom{n}{k} &= \frac{n!}{k!\,(n-k)!} \\ &= \binom{n}{n-k} An aligned derivation using the binomial coefficient \binom{n}{k} from 3.3. Align on =; the factorial form needs a thin space \, before (n-k)!.

Matrices

Matrices are a grid, so they use & between entries and \\ between rows — the same two characters as align*. The only choice is which delimiter you want around the grid, and \(\LaTeX\) bakes that into the environment name:

EnvironmentDelimiterOutput
pmatrixparentheses\begin{pmatrix} a & b \\ c & d \end{pmatrix}
bmatrixbrackets\begin{bmatrix} a & b \\ c & d \end{bmatrix}
BmatrixBraces\begin{Bmatrix} a & b \\ c & d \end{Bmatrix}
vmatrixvertical bars (determinant)\begin{vmatrix} a & b \\ c & d \end{vmatrix}
VmatrixV double bars (norm)\begin{Vmatrix} a & b \\ c & d \end{Vmatrix}
matrixno delimiter\begin{matrix} a & b \\ c & d \end{matrix}

The naming is mnemonic once you see it: the lowercase letter is the delimiter, and an uppercase letter means the “bigger” bracket of that pair — b for square brackets, B for curly braces; v for single bars, V for double bars. So vmatrix is exactly the notation for a determinant, and Vmatrix the double bars of a norm. For an ellipsis inside a matrix, use \cdots (horizontal), \vdots (vertical), and \ddots (diagonal). All of these need math mode, and unlike plain arrays they auto-size their brackets to the contents.

\begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix} Identity in parentheses: pmatrix, entries separated by &, rows by \\. \begin{bmatrix} a & b \\ c & d \end{bmatrix} Square brackets means bmatrix. Two rows of two entries. \begin{vmatrix} a & b \\ c & d \end{vmatrix} = ad - bc A determinant uses vertical bars: vmatrix. The right side is just ad - bc. \begin{pmatrix} x \\ y \\ z \end{pmatrix} A column vector is a one-column pmatrix: each entry on its own row, separated by \\ with no &. \begin{bmatrix} a_{11} & \cdots & a_{1n} \\ \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\ a_{n1} & \cdots & a_{nn} \end{bmatrix} A general matrix with ellipses: \cdots across, \vdots down, \ddots on the diagonal. Entries carry two-digit subscripts, so brace them: a_{11}.

Piecewise Definitions

A function defined by cases — and the brace that groups them — is the cases environment. Each line has the value, then &, then its condition, ended by \\. \(\LaTeX\) draws the single large left brace automatically:

|x| = \begin{cases}
  x & \text{if } x \geq 0 \\
  -x & \text{if } x < 0
\end{cases}

The conditions are words, so they sit inside \text{...} (see 6.3) — note the trailing space inside \text{if } so the condition does not collide with the math after it. The & here separates “value” from “condition” rather than two matrix columns, but it is the very same separator doing the very same job.

f(x) = \begin{cases} 1 & x > 0 \\ 0 & x \leq 0 \end{cases} Use cases. Value, then &, then condition; end the first line with \\. |x| = \begin{cases} x & \text{if } x \geq 0 \\ -x & \text{if } x < 0 \end{cases} The conditions are words, so wrap them in \text{if } (with a trailing space). Two cases, joined by \\. \operatorname{sgn}(x) = \begin{cases} 1 & x > 0 \\ 0 & x = 0 \\ -1 & x < 0 \end{cases} Three cases. The name on the left has no built-in command, so use \operatorname{sgn} from 2.2. (a+b)^2 &= a^2 + 2ab + b^2 \\ (a-b)^2 &= a^2 - 2ab + b^2 Two aligned identities. Each has &= and a braced square; the first line ends in \\. \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 2 \\ 3 & 4 \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} x \\ y \end{pmatrix} A matrix times a column vector: two pmatrix environments written side by side. \begin{vmatrix} a & b \\ c & d \end{vmatrix} = ad - bc Whiteboard challenge. Read the scribble and type its \(\LaTeX\). A determinant is a vmatrix (vertical bars), equal to ad - bc.

Annotations and Fine-Tuning

The last family of tools decorates and spaces what you have already built: accents over letters, braces that label a sub-expression, and the deliberate spacing that separates good typesetting from a wall of symbols.

Accents

An accent sits on top of its argument and takes exactly one argument; braces are optional over a single letter (\hat a works) but always safe. The catch is width: \hat, \tilde, and \bar are fixed-width and do not stretch, so each has a wide partner for arguments longer than one symbol.

AccentSingle letterOutputWide (several symbols)
Hat\hat{a}\hat{a}\widehat{abc}
Tilde\tilde{a}\tilde{a}\widetilde{abc}
Bar\bar{a}\bar{a}\overline{abc}
Vector\vec{a}\vec{a}\overrightarrow{AB}
Dot / double dot\dot{a} \ddot{a}\dot{a}\ \ddot{a}—

The difference is easy to see — the fixed accent stays one-letter-wide and looks cramped, while the wide version stretches to cover the whole argument:

\hat{abc}
\widehat{abc}

As a rule: one symbol, use the short accent; more than one, use the wide one. The \overline bar doubles as the notation for set complements and complex conjugates. One trap worth memorising: with vectors, put any subscript outside the accent — write \vec{v}_1 for \(\vec{v}_1\), not \vec{v_1}. This platform warns you if you get it backwards.

\hat{x} + \bar{y} A hat is \hat{...} and a bar is \bar{...}. \vec{v} = \vec{a} + \vec{b} Vector arrows use \vec{...} over each single letter. \overline{z} = \overline{a + bi} A bar over several symbols stretches, so use \overline{...} (not \bar) for the conjugate of a + bi. \widehat{ABC} A hat over more than one letter must stretch: \widehat{...}. \dot{x} = v, \quad \ddot{x} = a Newton's dot notation for time derivatives: \dot{x} and \ddot{x}, separated by a \quad gap. \vec{v}_1 + \vec{v}_2 Subscript goes outside the arrow: \vec{v}_1, never \vec{v_1}.

Braces and Labels

To label or group part of an expression, a horizontal brace can be drawn above or below it. \underbrace{a+b+c}_{\text{sum}} puts a brace under the terms with a subscript label: \(\underbrace{a+b+c}_{\text{sum}}\). Its mirror is \overbrace{...}^{...}, with the label as a superscript: \(\overbrace{1+2+\cdots+n}^{n\text{ terms}}\). The label is attached exactly like an ordinary subscript or superscript on the brace, which is why one uses _ and the other ^.

Closely related are \overset{above}{base} and \underset{below}{base}, which place one thing over or under another — handy for annotating a relation, as in a \overset{?}{=} b giving \(a \overset{?}{=} b\). These are finishing touches; reach for them only when an annotation genuinely aids the reader.

\underbrace{a + b + c}_{\text{sum}} Brace underneath with a labelled subscript: \underbrace{...}_{\text{sum}}. \overbrace{1 + 2 + \cdots + n}^{n \text{ terms}} Brace on top with a superscript label: \overbrace{...}^{...}. The running dots are \cdots; the label mixes a variable and the word "terms" via \text.

Spacing and Text

In math mode \(\LaTeX\) ignores the spaces you type and inserts its own based on what each symbol is, so pressing the space bar more does nothing. To adjust spacing you use explicit commands:

CommandSizeTypical use
\,Thin spaceBefore a differential \mathrm{d}x; inside set braces
\:Medium spaceOccasional fine-tuning
\;Thick spaceSeparating clauses in a formula
\quadWide gapBetween an equation and a side condition
\qquadDouble-wide gapLarge separation, e.g. listing cases on one line
\!Negative thin spacePulling two symbols slightly closer

The thin space \, is the one you will use most. To put real words inside math, wrap them in \text{...}: without it, x = 0 for all y becomes the italic letter-soup \(x = 0 for all y\) instead of \(x = 0 \text{ for all } y\). Mind the spaces — they must go inside the braces (\text{ for all }), because math mode eats the spaces on either side. This is the same \text you met in cases conditions and brace labels.

\int x^2 \, \mathrm{d}x The thin space before the differential is \,; the upright d is \mathrm{d}. x = 0 \text{ for all } y Words go inside \text{ for all } — keep the spaces inside the braces so the phrase does not collide with the math. a \quad b \qquad c A wide gap is \quad and a double-wide gap is \qquad. n \in \mathbb{Z}, \quad n > 0 A side condition set off by a \quad gap; the integers are \mathbb{Z} from 1.3. \hat{p} \approx \bar{x} A hat \hat{p} and a bar \bar{x}, joined by \approx. \lim_{n \to \infty} \left( 1 + \frac{1}{n} \right)^n = e A limit, auto-sized parentheses \left( ... \right) (see 4.2) around a fraction, all raised to ^n. f(x) = 0 \text{ for all } x \in \mathbb{R} The phrase sits in \text{ for all }; the reals are \mathbb{R} and membership is \in. \underbrace{1 + 1 + \cdots + 1}_{n \text{ times}} = n A labelled under-brace from 6.2: \underbrace{...}_{n \text{ times}}. The running dots are \cdots, and the label mixes a variable with a word via \text.

Common Errors and Further Reading

You now have everything needed for a clean problem set. This closing section is a checklist of the mistakes that trip up first-years — many of which this platform flags for you as you type — followed by a few conventions and where to go next.

Two of these deserve a fuller word. The double-superscript error x^2^3 is genuinely ambiguous — \(\LaTeX\) cannot tell whether you mean the tower \(x^{2^3}\) or \({x^2}^3\), so you must brace one level. And the rule about math mode cuts both ways: every symbol belongs inside \(...\) or \[...\], but every word inside math belongs in \text{...}. Forgetting either half is equally common.

Style Conventions

Beyond avoiding errors, a few habits make your work read like a mathematician's. Be consistent: pick \(\mathbb{Z}\) or \(\mathbf{Z}\) for the integers and stay with it. Prefer semantic commands to look-alikes — \mid for "divides" or "such that" rather than a bare |, \colon for a function colon, \to for a mapping arrow — because each carries the spacing its meaning deserves. Display any equation that is too tall or important to sit in a line, and keep punctuation as part of the sentence, inside the display. Finally, let \(\LaTeX\) do the spacing: resist the urge to nudge things with manual spaces unless something genuinely looks wrong.

Further Reading

This guide is deliberately a basic reference. When you outgrow it, these are the standard next stops. Overleaf's documentation is the most beginner-friendly, with a searchable article for nearly every topic. The Not So Short Introduction to LaTeX ("lshort") is the classic free book covering whole-document structure. When you have a symbol but not its name, Detexify lets you draw it and returns the command, and the Comprehensive LaTeX Symbol List catalogues thousands more. For questions, TeX Stack Exchange has almost certainly answered yours already. Since this platform renders with KaTeX, its support table tells you exactly which commands will display here.

One topic deliberately left out is TikZ, \(\LaTeX\)'s language for drawing figures — commutative diagrams, graphs, geometric pictures, and more. It is powerful and genuinely fun, but it is a small programming language in its own right and well beyond a basic notation guide. If a diagram is what you need, start from the TikZ & PGF manual or one of the many online galleries, and borrow liberally. For now, everything in the seven sections above is enough to write your summer's mathematics cleanly.
